Executive Summary

PULSE is a software-driven energy optimization platform that leverages Verizon's existing Asentria SiteBoss infrastructure to monitor and control HVAC, DC power plant, generator, and radio assets at macro cell sites — with zero new hardware required for Phase 1. By intelligently writing setpoints to communicating HVAC controllers already installed at ~23,873 macro sites, PULSE avoids peak demand charges, reduces energy consumption, and delivers actionable site intelligence across the fleet. The business case is clear: zero new hardware for Phase 1, a sub-12-month payback for Phase 2 cabling, and a proven platform that unlocks $3.5M+ in annual savings across all 23,873 communicating HVAC sites in the Verizon macro fleet.

Battery-Aware Intelligence

PULSE knows the battery type at each site and enforces safe temperature thresholds before writing any HVAC setpoint. The key insight: Verizon's published limits are continuous operating limits — not instantaneous trip points. Just like an HVAC deadband allows brief excursions before triggering action, PULSE applies a thermal deadband that permits a controlled 3°F exceedance during a 15-minute peak event, then immediately auto-restores. This is the core IP — not just raising a setpoint, but knowing when and by how much it is safe to do so.

Why 5% DoD Is Safe
VRLA batteries are rated for 200+ cycles at 100% DoD and 1,000+ cycles at 30% DoD. At 5% DoD, cycle life impact is negligible — equivalent to less than 1.5% of VRLA design life consumed per year of PULSE operation. The 45-minute cooldown between events ensures the string returns to full float before the next event fires. The 80% SoC gate is a hard block — PULSE will not trigger on a battery that hasn't recovered.
The Real VRLA Risk — and Why PULSE Avoids It
VRLA failure is driven by deep discharge below 1.83V/cell (44V on a 24-cell string) and by Partial State of Charge — repeatedly cycling without full recharge. PULSE's 50V termination voltage floor is 6V above the damage threshold. The SoC gate and cooldown timer together ensure every event starts from a full battery. The thermal guard ensures temperature-related aging is never compounded by a PULSE event.
Rectifier Curtailment Strategy

HVAC setpoint adjustment reduces cooling load. But PULSE has a second, independent demand lever — the DC power plant itself. Every Verizon cell site runs an N+1 rectifier configuration, meaning there is always at least one spare rectifier online. That spare capacity can be temporarily idled to cut AC input draw without any impact to network operation.

ABB Plant Controllers Confirmed — Full Peak Shaving Engine Already Built In
The ABB power plant controller (firmware v4.5.46, reachable at IPv6:8080) has a dedicated Peak Shaving Settings page with an Enable checkbox, configurable Termination Voltage, Manual Duration (0–480 minutes), and up to 3 repeating weekly schedule rules. PULSE either triggers the manual mode for real-time demand response, or writes the schedule rules to run autonomously during known peak hours. The plant already knows how to do this — PULSE provides the intelligence layer that decides when.

PULSE sends HTTP POST to ABB controller triggering "start peak shaving" or placing one rectifier (e.g. G11) into STANDBY. Remaining rectifiers handle full load — N+1 means one offline is always safe. AC input to that unit drops to zero. Batteries absorb any delta.
Post-Event — Auto-Restore
54.0V
Float voltage restored
After the 15-minute window, PULSE issues a second HTTP POST returning the rectifier from STANDBY to normal operation. The unit ramps back up within seconds, batteries recharge at their normal float rate. No manual intervention. No alarms. No network impact.
Why This Works — The Physics
A typical Verizon macro site draws 140–160A at 54V from the DC plant (~7.5–8.6 kW DC load), spread across 4–5 rectifier modules.

Placing one rectifier in STANDBY removes its entire AC draw from the meter. A single 25A rectifier module at 54V = ~1.35 kW DC, or roughly 1.5–1.7 kW AC including efficiency losses — gone for the duration of the peak window.

Batteries (fully charged at float) absorb the minor load delta for 15 minutes without stress or measurable discharge.

Combined with HVAC setpoint raise, total site demand reduction reaches 2.5–4 kW. At $10/kW/month that is $300–480/site/year from one monthly peak event.

  • ✓ Battery temperature within spec limit (pre-event read)

  • ! ABB Termination Voltage must be set to ≥42V before enabling — factory default is 40V (below Verizon EOV of 42V)

Key Differentiator
Most energy management platforms focus on HVAC setpoints alone. PULSE treats the entire site as a coordinated system — HVAC setpoint + rectifier float reduction + block heater scheduling all fire in concert during the 15-minute peak window. Each lever is independently safe; together they deliver the maximum achievable demand reduction from existing hardware, with zero network impact.
Control Architecture

PULSE uses three independent control paths — one per system. Each path has a different mechanism, a different confidence level, and a different set of prerequisites. Even if only the HVAC path is active at a site, PULSE still delivers measurable demand savings. Every additional path enabled stacks on top.
